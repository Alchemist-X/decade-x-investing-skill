# Risk and Position Sizing

> **Educational only. Not financial advice. Investing involves risk of loss. Position sizing decisions should be made with a licensed financial advisor who understands your complete financial situation.**

This framework addresses how much to allocate, not whether to allocate. A correct thesis sized incorrectly can still produce a bad outcome — through forced selling at a drawdown, behavioral panic, or concentration that triggers ruin-level losses. Conversely, an excellent thesis sized too timidly produces little incremental value versus simply holding an index.

The goal is calibrated conviction: size that is large enough to matter if you are right, small enough to survive if you are wrong.

---

## Principle 1 — Avoid Ruin First

The primary objective of position sizing is to ensure that no single position, no matter how wrong you are, can cause permanent impairment of your overall financial position. This is not a statement about expected returns — it is a constraint that governs all sizing decisions.

**The Kelly Criterion (educational illustration):**

The full Kelly fraction = Edge / Odds, where:
- Edge = your probability-weighted expected return above the risk-free rate
- Odds = the payoff ratio (how much you win vs. how much you lose)

In practice, most experienced practitioners use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly because:
- Edge estimates are uncertain — real edge is almost always smaller than estimated.
- Kelly assumes reinvestment at the same edge continuously, which is never true.
- Full Kelly produces drawdowns that are psychologically unbearable and that will force most investors to sell at the worst times.
- At half-Kelly, expected wealth growth is ~75% of full Kelly; at quarter-Kelly, it is ~50% of full Kelly. The psychic and behavioral cost reduction is worth the expected-return trade-off.

**Practical ruin-avoidance rules:**
- Size each position so that a 60–70% decline in that position (which is plausible for individual equities in a severe bear market) produces a loss you can absorb without material impact on your lifestyle, forced selling, or emotional destabilization.
- No single position should produce a portfolio loss exceeding your maximum tolerable single-year portfolio drawdown if it goes to zero.
- If you have to check the price of a position daily because of anxiety, it is sized too large for your psychological risk tolerance — even if your financial risk tolerance is higher.

## Principle 2 — Concentration vs. Diversification

The research literature is genuinely mixed on this — because the answer depends critically on the quality of the research underlying the concentrated positions.

**The research case for concentration:**
- Concentrated portfolios of high-conviction, thoroughly-researched positions have historically outperformed broadly diversified ones — conditional on the research being correct.
- Charlie Munger, Buffett, and other exceptional long-term investors argue that over-diversification dilutes the advantage of rigorous analysis.
- Owning 50+ positions you do not understand deeply is "diworsification" — diversifying away return without proportionally reducing risk.

**The research case for diversification:**
- Individual security picking skill is rare, difficult to sustain, and almost impossible to distinguish from luck without very long track records (15–20 years minimum).
- Black swan events, fraud, and technological disruption affect individual companies in ways that are not forecastable even with excellent analysis.
- A portfolio of 20–30 uncorrelated positions captures approximately 90% of the risk reduction of a fully diversified portfolio (Markowitz, empirically confirmed).
- For non-professional investors with limited research time, diversification is the rational default.

**Practical sizing framework by conviction level:**

| Conviction Level | Durability Score | Position Size Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Very high | 24–30/30 | 8–15% of investable portfolio | Requires wide moat + favorable valuation + strong megatrend alignment |
| High | 18–23/30 | 5–8% | Meaningful fragilities must be documented |
| Moderate | 12–17/30 | 3–5% | Size conservatively; thesis has real uncertainties |
| Speculative | Below 12/30 | 1–3% | Must tolerate total loss without portfolio impact |
| No single theme or sector | — | Max 30–35% aggregate | Regardless of individual conviction |

**Pre-position sizing questions:**
- If this position doubles, will I rebalance or let it ride? (Having an answer before size-in prevents reactive decision-making.)
- If this position falls 50%, will I add, hold, or sell? (If the answer is "sell," be honest about your true risk tolerance before sizing.)
- Does this position's size relative to my portfolio match my confidence level — and am I confident because of analysis or because of recent price appreciation?

## Principle 3 — Drawdown Tolerance and Time Horizon Matching

Every investment thesis has an implicit duration. A 10-year thesis should only be funded with capital you can genuinely lock away for 10 years. If you fund a 10-year thesis with capital you may need in 3 years, you are not a long-term investor — you are a short-term investor with long-term pretensions, and you will likely be forced to sell at the worst time.

**Drawdown expectation table (historical approximations — not guarantees):**

| Asset/Vehicle Type | Typical Max Drawdown in a Severe Bear Market |
|---|---|
| Individual small-cap equities | −70% to −90% |
| Individual large-cap equities | −40% to −70% |
| High-quality large-cap dividend payers | −30% to −50% |
| Broad equity index (e.g., S&P 500) | −50% to −57% (2008–2009 peak-to-trough) |
| High-yield bonds | −30% to −40% |
| Investment-grade bonds | −10% to −20% |
| Short-duration government bonds | −5% to −10% |
| Cash/money market | ~0% nominal (inflation risk is real) |

**Time-horizon matching questions (answer before sizing):**
1. If this position drops 60%, do I have other liquid assets to cover living expenses for 2+ years without selling this position?
2. If this position drops 60%, will I be able to hold — and potentially add — or will I sell at the trough?
3. Does the time horizon of this investment match my actual liquidity needs? A 10-year thesis should not be funded with capital earmarked for a home purchase or children's education within 5 years.
4. Does my expected return horizon match the thesis horizon? A catalyst-dependent thesis (regulatory approval, market share inflection) requires patience for the specific catalyst — not just patience for "the long run."

**Behavioral liquidity test:** Run through the specific scenario where this position is down 40% twelve months from now and has shown no signs of recovery. Describe, in writing, what you will do — and why. If you cannot write a coherent answer grounded in thesis logic (not hope), you are not yet ready to size this position.

## Principle 4 — Correlation and Portfolio-Level Thinking

Individual position sizing is incomplete without understanding portfolio-level correlation. A portfolio of 10 "diversified" positions that all move together in a risk-off event is effectively a single concentrated bet on market direction.

**Common correlation traps:**
- Owning 5 different AI semiconductor companies believing you are diversified. In a risk-off event, their correlation approaches 1.0 — you have a 5-position bet on AI semiconductor sentiment, not five uncorrelated bets.
- Owning international equities expecting diversification from US equity risk. International equity correlations with US equities have risen sharply during crises (2008, 2020) — precisely when diversification is most needed.
- Owning both long-duration bonds and long-duration equities (high P/E growth stocks). Both are sensitive to rate changes — rising rates hurt both simultaneously.
- Owning multiple "different" thematic ETFs that share the same underlying top holdings.

**Practical portfolio stress test:**
1. Identify your largest theme exposure (aggregate weight across all positions in that theme).
2. Apply a 40% decline to that theme.
3. What does your overall portfolio do? What is the total portfolio drawdown?
4. Do you have any position or asset class that is likely to be positively correlated to that decline, making it worse (e.g., high leverage + rising rates)?
5. Do you have any uncorrelated or inversely correlated ballast (short-duration fixed income, commodity exposure, true alternative strategies) — and is it sized meaningfully enough to matter?

**Correlation testing heuristic:** In a 2008-type scenario, assets that sold off together were: equities (all regions), high-yield bonds, real estate, commodities, emerging markets. Assets that held up or appreciated: US Treasury bonds (flight to quality), cash, short equity strategies, certain managed futures. Know which bucket each position falls into.

## Principle 5 — Rebalancing Discipline

At a 10-year horizon, rebalancing is a process question, not a timing question. The goal is to maintain intentional exposures as prices drift, not to time the market.

**Rebalancing trigger types:**

| Trigger | Rule | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Threshold-based | Rebalance when a position exceeds target weight by X% (e.g., a 10% target position that grows to 16%) | Systematic; avoids both under-trading and over-trading |
| Calendar-based | Annual review regardless of price movement | Simple; forces regular re-examination of thesis |
| Event-based (most important) | Rebalance when the original thesis changes materially | Thesis-driven; superior to price-driven |

**Critical distinction:**
- Rebalancing because a stock has risen significantly does **not** mean the thesis is wrong. A stock up 80% may still be cheap if the business has compounded faster than the price.
- Rebalancing because the durability score has declined — moat has narrowed, leverage has increased, disruption has materialized, management has destroyed capital — is thesis-driven and is always the more important question.

**Practical rebalancing questions at each review:**
1. Has any key assumption from the decision journal changed materially?
2. Has the durability score changed meaningfully since entry?
3. Has position size drifted beyond the original target weight due to appreciation? Does the new, larger weight still reflect your conviction level?
4. Has a new position emerged that deserves more capital — and does funding it require trimming this one?

## Principle 6 — Behavioral Pitfall Inventory

The largest source of long-horizon underperformance is behavioral, not analytical. Kahneman, Thaler, and the behavioral finance literature consistently document that investors with good analytical frameworks still underperform systematic processes because they override their process at the worst times.

**Full behavioral pitfall inventory:**

| Pitfall | Description | How It Manifests in Long-Horizon Investing | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Narrative attachment | Holding a thesis because you publicly argued for it or emotionally identified with it | Refusing to update when falsifiers are triggered; spending energy on counter-arguments rather than counter-evidence | Pre-commit to specific falsifiers in writing before entry; force yourself to re-read the bear case at each review |
| Recency bias | Extrapolating recent performance indefinitely | After 3 good years, assuming the next 7 will be equally good; after a bad year, catastrophizing | Review base rates before reviewing price charts; always start with the long-run historical distribution |
| Loss aversion | The pain of a loss is ~2x the pleasure of an equivalent gain | Holding losers past the point where the thesis has broken, to avoid "realizing" the loss | Make all decisions based on the forward thesis and forward expected return, not the entry price |
| Overconfidence | Concentrating excessively after early success | After a position doubles, treating that as evidence of edge and adding more aggressively | Maintain position-size discipline regardless of P&L trajectory; a position going up is not evidence the thesis was correct — it may just be multiple expansion |
| Action bias | Feeling compelled to do something during volatility to feel in control | Over-trading in response to news, price moves, or market anxiety | Pre-set rules for when you will and will not trade; use the decision journal review schedule to create structure |
| Illiquidity trap | Owning small-cap or micro-cap positions so large relative to average daily volume that you cannot exit without moving the price against yourself | A $50M position in a stock with $1M daily volume takes 50+ trading days to exit, during which the price may decline | Size positions relative to average daily volume; a rough rule is that the position should be exitable within 5–10 trading days at normal volume |
| Anchoring | Using the purchase price, 52-week high, or an analyst's old price target as a reference point for valuation | "It was $200, now it's $120 — it's cheap!" (it may not be) | Evaluate all decisions based on current intrinsic value estimate, not historical prices or targets |
| Confirmation bias | Seeking information that confirms the existing thesis | Reading only the bull case; dismissing bear arguments without engaging with them | Actively seek out the most credible bear arguments; simulate a "red team" that is trying to disprove the thesis |
| Hindsight rationalization | Reconstructing your reasoning after outcomes are known to make it look more prescient | "I knew that would happen" — obscures what you actually knew at the time | The decision journal is the primary mitigation: it records actual reasoning before outcomes are known |

## Principle 7 — Pre-Mortem and Position Exit Conditions

Set exit conditions before you enter the position. Deciding when to exit while holding a position is emotionally compromised — you are either anchored to an entry price (refusing to sell a loser) or emotionally attached to a winner.

**Pre-mortem exercise:**
Before entering any position, write a one-paragraph description of how this investment loses money. Make it specific, not vague ("the business deteriorates"). What exactly happens, in what sequence, and on what timeline? This exercise:
1. Forces you to think about the downside as concretely as the upside.
2. Surfaces whether you have considered the primary risk vectors.
3. Provides a template for recognizing the scenario as it unfolds.

**Exit condition types:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Thesis break | A key assumption is falsified | "If churn rate exceeds 15% for two consecutive quarters" |
| Valuation | Price exceeds bull case intrinsic value estimate | "If P/E exceeds 40x on normalized earnings" |
| Portfolio management | Position weight exceeds maximum target due to appreciation | "Trim if position exceeds 15% of portfolio" |
| Time stop | Thesis has not developed in expected timeframe | "Reassess if there is no evidence of inflection in 24 months" |

Do not use a price-based stop-loss as a thesis exit — it conflates price movement with thesis movement. A stock down 30% may be a better thesis than when you bought it; a stock up 50% may be a broken thesis in disguise.
