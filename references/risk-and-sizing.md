# Risk and Position Sizing

> **Educational only. Not financial advice. Investing involves risk of loss. Position sizing decisions should be made with a licensed financial advisor who understands your complete financial situation.**

This framework addresses how much to allocate, not whether to allocate. A correct thesis sized incorrectly can still produce a bad outcome — through forced selling at a drawdown, behavioral panic, or concentration that triggers ruin-level losses.

---

## Principle 1 — Avoid Ruin First

The primary objective of position sizing is to ensure that no single position, no matter how wrong you are, can cause permanent impairment of your overall financial position.

**The Kelly Criterion (educational illustration):**
The full Kelly fraction = (Edge / Odds). In practice, most practitioners use half-Kelly or quarter-Kelly because:
- Edge estimates are uncertain.
- Kelly assumes you can reinvest at the same edge, which is usually not true.
- Full Kelly produces drawdowns that are psychologically unbearable and may force premature exit.

**Practical rule:** If you would be forced to sell a position because of a 50% drawdown in that position (due to margin calls, emotional distress, or liquidity needs), the position is too large. Size it so a 50% decline is painful but survivable without forced action.

---

## Principle 2 — Concentration vs. Diversification

**The research case for concentration:**
- Concentrated portfolios of high-conviction, thoroughly-researched positions have historically outperformed broadly diversified ones — when the research is correct.
- Diversification across many positions you do not understand deeply is "diworsification."

**The research case for diversification:**
- Individual security picking skill is rare and difficult to sustain.
- Black swan events, fraud, and disruption affect individual companies in ways that are not forecastable.
- A portfolio of 20–30 uncorrelated positions captures ~90% of the risk reduction of a fully diversified portfolio.

**Practical framework:**
- High conviction, wide moat, high durability score: maximum single-position size 10–15% of investable portfolio.
- Moderate conviction: 5–8%.
- Speculative or early-stage: 1–3% (limited to total loss without portfolio impact).
- No single sector or theme: maximum 30–35% of portfolio regardless of individual conviction.

## Principle 3 — Drawdown Tolerance and Time Horizon Matching

**Drawdown expectation table (historical approximations, not guarantees):**

| Asset class | Typical max drawdown in a severe bear market |
|---|---|
| Individual small-cap equities | -70% to -90% |
| Individual large-cap equities | -40% to -70% |
| Broad equity index (e.g., S&P 500) | -50% to -57% |
| High-yield bonds | -30% to -40% |
| Investment-grade bonds | -10% to -20% |

**Questions to ask before sizing:**
1. If this position drops 60%, do I have other assets to cover living expenses for 2+ years without selling?
2. If this position drops 60%, will I be able to hold — and potentially add — or will I sell at the bottom?
3. Does the time horizon of this investment match my actual liquidity needs? A 10-year thesis should not be funded with capital you may need in 3 years.

---

## Principle 4 — Correlation and Portfolio-Level Thinking

Individual position sizing is incomplete without understanding portfolio-level correlation.

Common correlation traps:
- Owning 5 different AI semiconductor companies thinking you are diversified — you are not; they have near-1.0 correlation in a risk-off event.
- Owning international equities that have converged in correlation with US equities during crises.
- Owning both long-duration bonds and long-duration equities (high P/E growth stocks) — both reprice sharply when rates rise.

**Practical check:**
- In a scenario where your largest theme drops 40%, what does your overall portfolio do?
- Do you have any position or asset class that is likely to be positively correlated to that decline, making it worse?
- Do you have any hedge or uncorrelated position that provides ballast?

## Principle 5 — Rebalancing Discipline

At a 10-year horizon, rebalancing is a process question, not a timing question.

**Rebalancing rules to consider:**
- Threshold-based: rebalance when a position exceeds your target weight by X% (e.g., rebalance if a 10% target position grows to 15%).
- Calendar-based: review and rebalance annually regardless of price movement.
- Event-based: rebalance when the original thesis changes materially (not when the price changes).

**Important distinction:** Rebalancing because a stock has risen does not mean the thesis is wrong. Rebalancing because the durability score has declined — moat narrowed, leverage increased, disruption materialized — is thesis-driven and more important.

## Principle 6 — Behavioral Pitfall Inventory

The largest source of long-horizon underperformance is behavioral, not analytical.

| Pitfall | Description | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Narrative attachment | Holding a thesis because you publicly argued for it, not because the evidence supports it | Pre-commit to falsifiers (see decision journal) |
| Recency bias | Extrapolating recent performance into the future | Review base rates before reviewing price charts |
| Loss aversion | Holding losers too long to avoid realizing a loss | Make decisions based on forward thesis, not entry price |
| Overconfidence | Concentrating excessively after early success | Maintain position size discipline regardless of P&L |
| Action bias | Over-trading to feel in control | Set rules for when you will and will not trade |
| Illiquidity trap | Owning small-cap positions so large you cannot exit | Size positions relative to average daily volume |
| Anchoring | Using a stock's 52-week high or purchase price as a reference | Evaluate based on intrinsic value, not price history |
