# Megatrends Checklist

> **Educational only. Not financial advice. Investing involves risk of loss.**

Use this checklist to evaluate whether a theme qualifies as a durable secular megatrend or a cyclical/narrative-driven fad. Work through every category. Document your answers. A complete pass takes 30–60 minutes of genuine skepticism.

---

## Quick Orientation: Secular vs. Cyclical vs. Fad

| Type | Typical Duration | Driven By | Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secular megatrend | 15–40 years | Structural demographic, technological, or geopolitical forces | Persists through recessions; not rate-sensitive |
| Cyclical trend | 3–10 years | Credit cycles, inventory cycles, capex cycles | Strongly correlated with GDP and rates |
| Narrative fad | 1–3 years | Investor sentiment, media, hype cycles | Rapid multiple expansion with thin underlying fundamentals |

**Base rate to anchor against:** Of all investment themes that generated top-quartile flows in any given 5-year period, roughly 60–70% underperformed the broad market over the subsequent 10 years. Excitement is not evidence.

## Category 1 — Demographics

A demographic megatrend is driven by changes in population size, age structure, or migration that are largely locked in for the next decade. Demographic shifts are among the most forecastable forces in investing because births and deaths that already happened are immutable.

**Prompts:**
- Is this driven by an aging population, urbanization, or a growing middle class in a specific region?
- Are the relevant cohorts (consumers, workers, patients) increasing or decreasing in number — by how much, over what horizon?
- What UN or government demographic data supports this? What does the pessimistic demographic scenario look like?
- What is the lag between the demographic shift and its economic manifestation? (E.g., a birth-rate-driven education thesis requires 5–10 years to materialize in enrollment data.)

**Rigorous heuristic:** Demographic tailwinds are most powerful when (a) the cohort size is large, (b) the behavioral change is already underway (not merely predicted), and (c) no plausible policy can reverse the shift within a decade.

**Base rates:**
- Global working-age population growth is decelerating. UN medium-variant projects net additions of ~500M working-age people globally 2020–2030, but concentrated in Sub-Saharan Africa — not necessarily where capital markets are deepest.
- US 65+ population grows from ~18% to ~21% of total by 2030 (locked-in births pre-1965).

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What demographic reversal (birth rate spike, migration policy change, life-extension breakthrough, mass automation displacing the targeted cohort) would undermine this trend within 10 years? Could a policy intervention (e.g., pension reform, immigration expansion) offset the assumed demographic pressure?

Score: `__/5` (0 = not demographic; 3 = partially demographic; 5 = strongly demographic with locked-in, quantified data)

## Category 2 — Energy Transition

**Prompts:**
- Is this thesis tied to the shift from fossil fuels to electrification, hydrogen, or efficiency?
- What is the current cost curve trajectory for the enabling technology (solar, battery, grid storage)? Is it following Wright's Law (cost declines of ~20% per doubling of cumulative production)?
- What does the incumbent energy industry do in response? How much capital is directed at defense — and does that capital extend or compress the runway for the new entrant?
- Is the thesis dependent on a specific policy regime (IRA tax credits, EU carbon pricing) that could be reversed?

**Rigorous heuristic:** Seek cost-curve crossover points. Solar levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) crossed fossil fuel LCOE in most markets by 2020. The next crossover events (long-duration storage, green hydrogen, EV TCO) are what to track for remaining runway.

**Base rates:**
- Solar module cost declined ~90% per decade since 1980 — Wright's Law in action. Battery costs declined ~90% 2010–2023. These are among the most robust technology cost curves in history.
- Energy transitions historically take 40–60 years (coal → oil, oil → gas). Current transition may be faster due to policy acceleration, but betting on 10-year completion is still aggressive.

**Disconfirmation prompt:** Under what policy reversal (fossil fuel subsidies restored, carbon tax repealed), technology plateau (battery energy density stalls, grid integration costs surge), or resource constraint (lithium, cobalt, copper supply bottleneck) does this trend stall or reverse? What is the stranded-asset risk if the transition is faster or slower than modeled?

Score: `__/5`

## Category 3 — Compute and AI

**Prompts:**
- Is this thesis tied to continued Moore's Law dynamics, AI capability scaling, or semiconductor demand?
- What is the current utilization rate of the enabling infrastructure? Is this a build-out phase (supply exceeds demand = price compression risk) or a digestion phase (demand absorbs supply = stable pricing)?
- Has this sector shown a pattern of boom-bust capital cycles? (Telecom 1999–2001: $1T+ in fiber laid, 90%+ valuation collapse. Cloud 2020–2022: massive infrastructure build followed by snap-back correction.)
- Is the AI thesis at the infrastructure layer (picks and shovels, durable across application winners), the model layer (winner-take-most dynamics), or the application layer (most competitive, most commoditized over time)?

**Rigorous heuristic:** Separate "the technology is real and important" from "this specific company benefits durably." Most value in past platform shifts accrued to a small number of incumbents and a few new entrants — not to the hundreds of companies claiming exposure. Ask: which layer of the stack captures the most durable margin?

**Base rates:**
- Semiconductor revenue has grown at ~7% CAGR over 30 years with significant cyclicality (peak-to-trough declines of 30–50% in down cycles).
- Of the top 20 AI/internet companies by market cap in 2000, fewer than 5 were still top-20 by 2010.
- Hyperscaler capex guidance for AI infrastructure 2024–2027: $600B+ cumulative — but "build" and "monetize" are not synonymous.

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What regulatory (chip export controls, AI liability legislation), geopolitical (Taiwan risk, US-China decoupling), or architectural shift (alternative computing paradigms — neuromorphic, quantum, optical) could disrupt the assumed trajectory? If the model layer commoditizes (as happened with web search economics after 2010), what happens to infrastructure valuations?

Score: `__/5`

## Category 4 — Biotechnology and Healthcare

**Prompts:**
- Is the thesis grounded in platform technology (mRNA, CRISPR, GLP-1 mechanisms) with expanding applications — or a single product/approval?
- What is the regulatory pathway risk? What does the historical base rate of clinical trial success look like in this indication?
- Is the payer dynamic (insurance, government, out-of-pocket) supportive over 10 years?
- What is the competitive response? If a platform proves lucrative, how quickly will second/third movers erode pricing?

**Rigorous heuristic:** Platform durability vs. single-product risk is the defining distinction. mRNA as a platform demonstrated proof-of-concept across multiple vaccine and therapeutic applications — that is platform evidence. A single GLP-1 drug approval is single-product evidence. Platform evidence is more durable; single-product evidence is event risk.

**Base rates (clinical trials — rough historical averages):**
- Phase I → Approval overall: ~10–15% (all indications)
- Oncology specifically: ~5–7% Phase I → Approval
- CNS disorders: ~7–9%
- Infectious disease: ~20–25% (boosted by COVID experience)
- Companies with no approved products at time of investment: historically 70–80% fail to reach approval within 10 years

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What safety signal (late-stage trial failure, post-market adverse events), patent cliff (biosimilar entry compressing revenue 60–80% within 2 years of expiry), drug pricing reform (IRA negotiation expanding to more drugs), or biosimilar dynamic would materially compress the opportunity within 5 years?

Score: `__/5`

## Category 5 — Geopolitical and Supply Chain Restructuring

**Prompts:**
- Is this driven by reshoring, friend-shoring, or decoupling trends with bipartisan/multi-government support?
- How much of the trend is already priced into capex announcements vs. actual shovel-in-ground activity? (Capex announcements are press releases; permits, ground-breaking, and production ramp are evidence.)
- Are there infrastructure dependencies (skilled labor, permitting timelines, raw materials, grid capacity) that could delay or kill this thesis?
- Is this trend durable across potential changes in US or EU political leadership?

**Rigorous heuristic:** Distinguish announced intent from economic inevitability. Reshoring is economically logical only when domestic production costs are competitive (or nearly so) with offshore. Subsidy-dependent reshoring is policy risk, not structural advantage.

**Base rates:**
- US CHIPS Act ($52B) semiconductor reshoring: TSMC Arizona originally targeted 2024 production, slipped to 2025–2026 due to skilled labor shortages. Cost premium vs. Taiwan: estimated 30–50%. History suggests 5–10 year lag between policy announcement and production at scale.
- Prior US manufacturing reshoring campaigns (1980s, 2000s) largely failed to reverse offshoring trends. Current campaign has more bipartisan support and national security framing — but cost differentials remain structurally challenging.

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What geopolitical rapprochement (US-China trade deal, Taiwan de-escalation) or policy reversal (administration change, subsidy cuts, WTO ruling) would re-globalize the supply chains your thesis depends on? What is the company's margin profile without the subsidy?

Score: `__/5`

## Category 6 — Financial / Macro Tailwinds

Be cautious here. Financial tailwinds (low rates, credit expansion, multiple expansion) are cyclical, not secular. A great business at the wrong price, bought on a financial tailwind, can still produce a poor decade-long return.

**Prompts:**
- Does your thesis depend on a sustained low-rate environment? If rates normalize or rise, does the thesis survive?
- Are you confusing a long bull market in an asset class with a structural megatrend?
- What was the base rate of return for this asset class over the previous 30 years, across multiple rate cycles?
- Is the current valuation level for this sector in the top or bottom quartile of historical ranges? What does mean-reversion risk look like?

**Rigorous heuristic:** If the thesis works primarily because of multiple expansion (P/E, EV/EBITDA going up), that is not a business thesis — it is a sentiment bet. Durable returns come from earnings growth and cash return, not multiple re-rating.

**Base rates:**
- US 10-year Treasury yield 2010–2021 (ZIRP era): 0.5–3.0%. Post-2022 normalization: 4.0–5.0%. Many 2020–2021 thematic investments were implicitly underwriting continued low rates.
- P/E multiple expansion contributed ~30% of total S&P 500 return 2010–2020. Earnings and dividends contributed the rest. Multiple expansion is not reliably repeatable.

**Disconfirmation prompt:** If interest rates are 2 percentage points higher in 5 years, does the thesis still hold? What is the sensitivity of the company's valuation to a 100bps increase in discount rate? (High-duration growth stocks can see 30–40% valuation compression from this alone.)

Score: `__/5` (high score = NOT rate-dependent; low score = heavily rate-dependent — lower is worse here)

## Fad Detection Prompts

Run these regardless of category. Be ruthless.

1. **Narrative test:** Can you explain this trend to a skeptic using only data, not story? If the explanation requires "everyone knows that..." or "it's obvious that..." — flag it as narrative, not thesis.
2. **Crowding test:** What percentage of investor surveys, fund flows, and media coverage are already bullish on this theme? High crowding (>70% bullish in surveys) historically predicts underperformance at 3–5 year horizons. The trade that feels obvious is often already in prices.
3. **History test:** Has this theme appeared before under a different name and disappointed? (E.g., "cleantech 1.0" in 2007–2009 — same secular tailwinds, different vintage, most companies went to zero. "E-commerce revolution" in 1999 — real trend, wrong prices, most investors lost money even as the trend proved correct.)
4. **Supply response test:** If this opportunity is so large, why isn't capital flooding in to compete it away? If it is flooding in — what is the timeline to oversupply and margin compression? Track private market funding as a leading indicator of future public market competition.
5. **Time horizon test:** Would this trend have been predicted confidently 10 years ago? If not, why is a 10-year prediction reliable now? What information advantage do you have that the broad market is not pricing?
6. **Contrarian comfort test:** Can you articulate, in good faith, the best version of the bear case? If the bear case sounds stupid or uninformed to you, you may have narrative attachment rather than analysis.
