## Overview

This document walks through a complete application of the decade-investing framework, step by step, using the fictional company **NovaCure Industrial Systems (NCIS)** as the subject. NCIS is a fictional B2B software and services company that helps manufacturers optimize energy usage in heavy industrial plants using AI-based monitoring and control systems.

The purpose of this example is to show *how* to use each reference file in sequence, what good and poor answers look like, and what the synthesis looks like at the end. Read through this before your first real analysis — then come back after each step to compare your work.

## The Prompt That Started This Analysis

"NovaCure Industrial Systems is a $4B market-cap software company that helps steel mills, cement plants, and chemical manufacturers cut energy costs by 15–25% using AI. Revenue is $320M, growing 22% YoY. It's been public for 3 years. Should I research it as a 10-year thesis?"

*Note: Our role here is educational — we apply the framework, surface the evidence and gaps, and document the analysis. We do not make a buy/sell recommendation.*

---

## Step 1 — Frame the Thesis (2 sentences)

**Before running any checklist, write the thesis:**

> NovaCure provides AI-driven energy management software to heavy industrial manufacturers facing mandatory decarbonization regulation and rising energy costs. If regulatory pressure on industrial emissions continues to tighten and NovaCure's energy-savings ROI remains compelling, its embedded customer base and switching-cost moat should allow it to compound revenue and expand margins over the next decade.

**What needs to be true:**
1. Decarbonization regulation on heavy industry continues to tighten (politically durable).
2. NovaCure's software continues to deliver measurable 15–25% energy savings.
3. Switching costs remain high once the software is integrated into plant control systems.
4. Energy prices remain elevated enough that the ROI case is compelling without subsidy.
5. A better-funded competitor does not replicate the core functionality at lower cost.

**Falsifiers (specific, early-detectable):**
1. Net Revenue Retention falls below 100% for two consecutive quarters — suggests customers are not expanding or are churning.
2. A major industrial automation incumbent (e.g., Siemens, Honeywell, Rockwell) announces a directly competing product that enters pilots at three or more NovaCure's existing customers.
3. Major decarbonization regulation (EU ETS, US EPA industrial standards) is rolled back or significantly weakened in core markets, removing the regulatory tailwind.

## Step 3 — Durability Rubric Scores (NCIS example)

*Open: references/durability-rubric.md*

### Dimension 1 — Competitive Moat

NovaCure's product integrates at the control-system level (DCS/SCADA layer) inside customer plants. Integration takes 6–12 months and involves training the AI model on plant-specific operational data. Once integrated, the cost of switching to a competitor involves: re-integration time, loss of 2–4 years of historical optimization data, operational risk during transition.

Evidence supporting moat:
- Net Revenue Retention reported at 118% last year — strong expanding customer footprint.
- Gross margin: 72% and stable over 3 years of public history.
- ROIC (imputed): ~14% — above cost of capital but not yet exceptional.
- Pricing: company has taken 4–6% price increases annually with no reported customer pushback.

Weaknesses:
- Only 3 years of public track record — moat durability not yet proven through a full economic cycle.
- ROIC is above cost of capital but not wide-moat territory (typically 20%+ is the signal).

**Score: 3/5** — Real switching costs, good early evidence, but too short a track record to assign 4–5. Moat is plausible but not yet proven through a full downturn.

**Disconfirmation:** Siemens Digital Industries or Rockwell Automation adding AI energy optimization to their existing DCS platforms would significantly reduce switching costs (customers are already on their control infrastructure). This is the primary moat-threat.

---

### Dimension 2 — TAM and Runway

Heavy industrial manufacturing (steel, cement, chemicals, glass, paper) globally spends approximately $1.8T/year on energy. A 15–25% efficiency gain on even 5% of that market is a ~$14–22B opportunity. NovaCure's current $320M revenue implies roughly 2% penetration of a conservatively sized $15B TAM.

At 22% revenue growth: approximately 9–10 years to reach 10% TAM penetration at current TAM size. TAM itself is likely growing (more industrial plants globally, rising energy costs per unit of output).

**Score: 4/5** — Genuine large TAM with low penetration. Long runway before saturation is a real concern. Some caution warranted: B2B sales cycles in heavy industry are long, and TAM capture requires regulatory changes in some geographies (e.g., Chinese steel mills, Indian cement).

---

### Dimension 3 — Secular Tailwind Alignment

Energy efficiency in heavy industry has dual tailwinds: (a) decarbonization mandates (EU ETS, US Clean Air Act), (b) structural energy price elevation post-2021. Both push industrial operators to cut energy intensity. NovaCure's product addresses the core problem directly.

**Score: 5/5** — Tight, dual-anchored alignment with the energy transition megatrend.

---

### Dimension 4 — Balance Sheet Resilience

NCIS has $180M in cash, no debt, and generates $42M in FCF (13% FCF margin). FCF is growing; the company could sustain operations for 4+ years at current burn even with zero revenue (hypothetical). No debt maturity risk.

**Score: 5/5** — Fortress balance sheet for a $4B company. This is a strong asset if a recession or credit crunch occurs while competitors are over-leveraged.

---

### Dimension 5 — Management and Capital Allocation

CEO founded the company; owns 8% of shares (purchased, not optioned). Management has made two small acquisitions (both tuck-in sensor hardware companies, total consideration ~$40M) that appear integrated and accretive. Compensation: stock vesting over 4 years. No aggressive buybacks yet (company is growing; reinvestment is appropriate).

Capital allocation evidence is limited by 3-year public track record. Founder-led with meaningful equity ownership is a positive signal. No major capital allocation errors yet.

**Score: 3/5** — Positive early signals, limited track record. Assign "average" until evidence accumulates over a full cycle.

---

### Dimension 6 — Disruption Risk

Primary disruption vectors:
1. **Incumbent automation vendors** (Siemens, Honeywell, Rockwell) add AI energy optimization to their existing DCS platforms. These vendors have existing customer relationships and control hardware in the same plants. This is the highest-probability disruption risk.
2. **AI platform commoditization:** As generic AI tooling improves, customers may build in-house energy optimization models rather than buying specialized software.
3. **Open-source industrial AI:** Several energy-focused open-source projects (e.g., industrial RL toolkits) could reduce the barrier for in-house development.

Mitigants:
- NovaCure's 4 years of plant-specific training data per customer creates a proprietary data moat that generic tooling cannot replicate quickly.
- Integration at the DCS layer is deep — requires NCIS engineers on-site for months.
- Incumbents have historically been slow to move into software (cultural and business model inertia).

Given that the incumbent threat is real but historically slow-moving, and the data moat is genuine but not impenetrable, this is a moderate disruption risk.

**Score: 3/5** — Real but currently manageable. Monitor: any major incumbent entering serious pilots at NovaCure's customer base would lower this to 1–2 rapidly.

## Step 4 — Valuation Sanity Check (NCIS example)

*Open: references/valuation-sanity.md*

**Key inputs (all fictional):**
- Market cap: $4,000M
- Revenue: $320M
- FCF: $42M
- FCF margin: 13%
- Owner earnings (FCF − SBC estimated at $18M): $24M
- Owner earnings yield: 24 / 4000 = **0.6%**
- Current 10-year US Treasury yield (illustrative): 4.5%

**Immediate flag:** Owner earnings yield of 0.6% is far below the risk-free rate of 4.5%. This means the market is pricing in very substantial future growth — the current price requires high-growth assumptions to generate an adequate return.

**Reverse DCF implied growth:**
At 8% discount rate, 3% terminal growth, and $24M current owner earnings, what growth rate justifies a $4,000M market cap?

Working the math: Approximately **28% revenue CAGR for 10 years**, improving FCF margins from 13% to 25%, is needed to justify $4,000M at these discount rate assumptions. This is in the top 3–5% of historical outcomes for B2B software companies.

**Three-Scenario Analysis:**

| Scenario | Revenue CAGR | Terminal FCF Margin | Intrinsic Value Estimate | Probability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bear | 5% (growth stalls — regulation + competition) | 10% | ~$800M (~$6.40/share equiv.) | 25% |
| Base | 18% (solid execution, moderate competition) | 20% | ~$2,400M (~$19.20/share equiv.) | 50% |
| Bull | 28% (market penetration accelerates, pricing power) | 28% | ~$4,800M (~$38.40/share equiv.) | 25% |
| **Expected Value** | | | **~$2,600M** | **100%** |

*Share price equivalents are illustrative only — they use a simplified share count. Do not use these as targets.*

**Margin of safety at current market cap ($4,000M) vs. expected value ($2,600M):** Negative — current price is approximately 55% *above* expected value.

**Bear case:** A 25% probability scenario implies an ~80% decline from current price. This is not survivable at any meaningful position size.

**Interpretation:** At $4B market cap, NCIS appears to price in nearly the bull case. The margin of safety is negative — you are paying for the best outcome already. This does not mean the company is a bad business. It means the current price does not offer a margin of safety for a new long-term investor.

**What would change the math:** If price declined to approximately $2,000M market cap (−50%), the base case would offer a margin of safety of ~20%, and the expected value would roughly match the price. At that level, the risk-reward improves meaningfully.

## Step 5 — Risk and Sizing

*Open: references/risk-and-sizing.md*

**Given the valuation analysis:**
The thesis is genuine — the business quality is real, the tailwind is real. But the current price offers minimal margin of safety. Sizing should reflect this.

**Hypothetical scenario A — Investing at current price:**
If an investor chose to enter at current price despite the valuation concern (e.g., believing in the bull case with high conviction), the appropriate size given the durability score of 23/30 and the valuation concern would be:

- Maximum position: 3–5% of investable portfolio (down from the 8–10% that the durability score alone might suggest, adjusted downward for the valuation premium)
- Rationale: The bear case implies an 80% drawdown on the position — at a 4% portfolio weight, that is a 3.2% portfolio loss. Painful but survivable.
- The thin margin of safety makes this closer to "speculative at current price" than "high conviction."

**Hypothetical scenario B — Setting a target entry:**
More disciplined approach: monitor the business, wait for either a valuation correction (price approaches expected value) or a thesis inflection (evidence that falsifiers are moving favorably), and enter with a larger position at a better price.

**Correlation note:** A position in an industrial software company with energy-transition tailwind would be correlated to: other clean-tech positions, other software positions, and sentiment around industrial capex. If an investor already owns significant energy-transition or software exposure, this adds correlation without much diversification benefit.

**Behavioral pitfalls to flag for this specific company:**
- **Narrative attachment:** The energy-transition story is compelling and topical. Easy to become attached to the narrative and overlook the valuation concern.
- **Recency bias:** 22% revenue growth for 3 years creates recency bias that this rate will continue. Base rates say it almost certainly will not at this scale and valuation.

## What This Example Demonstrates

1. **Megatrend scoring is not binary.** A 15/30 score on a focused industrial-software company is reasonable — the company is not exposed to every megatrend. The important thing is identifying the primary anchor (energy transition, 5/5) and flagging its disconfirmation risk.

2. **Durability scoring can be positive even when moat is not fully proven.** A 23/30 score reflects real business quality with appropriate uncertainty discounting for the short track record. It does not mean "avoid" — it means "size conservatively and watch the fragility dimensions."

3. **Valuation can override a good thesis.** NCIS is a genuinely interesting business. But paying 55% above expected value for it is a poor process decision regardless of business quality. The framework's job is to surface this disconnect — not to rationalize it away because the story is compelling.

4. **The decision journal forces discipline.** Setting explicit falsifiers, a review schedule, and valuation triggers before entering the position creates a structure that helps distinguish "thesis breaking" from "price going down."

5. **Good process does not guarantee good outcomes.** Even if all the analysis above is correct, external events (regulation change, competitor entry, management error) could cause this investment to fail. Process reduces the frequency and severity of mistakes — it does not eliminate uncertainty.
