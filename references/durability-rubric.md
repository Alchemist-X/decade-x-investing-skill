# Durability Rubric

> **Educational only. Not financial advice. Investing involves risk of loss.**

Score a specific asset, company, or investment vehicle on six dimensions for 10-year durability. Each dimension is scored 0–5. The rubric surfaces where the thesis is strong and where it is fragile — it does not produce a buy/sell signal.

---

## Scoring Scale (applied to each dimension)

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 0 | Severely negative — material threat to the thesis |
| 1 | Weak — significant concern, limited mitigant |
| 2 | Below average — some concern, partial mitigant |
| 3 | Average — reasonable but not differentiated |
| 4 | Above average — clearly positive, well-supported |
| 5 | Exceptional — rare, durable, well-documented advantage |

---

## Dimension 1 — Competitive Moat

A moat is a structural advantage that allows sustained above-average returns on capital over time.

Moat types to assess:
- **Switching costs:** How painful is it for customers to leave? (E.g., ERP software, payment rails, embedded industrial systems)
- **Network effects:** Does the product become more valuable as more people use it? Is the network winner-take-all or fragmented?
- **Cost advantage:** Is there a structural cost advantage from scale, location, or process that competitors cannot replicate quickly?
- **Intangible assets:** Patents with clear expiry profiles, regulatory licenses, brand with pricing power (not mere recognition)?
- **Efficient scale:** Is the market too small to attract a second competitor (regional utilities, niche B2B platforms)?

Questions:
- What has the gross margin and return on invested capital (ROIC) trend looked like over 5–10 years?
- Has the company maintained pricing power through at least one recession?
- Can you name the three most dangerous potential entrants and explain why they haven't succeeded yet?

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What would cause this moat to narrow within 5 years (regulatory change, open-source alternative, platform shift, new entrant with 10x better cost structure)?

Score: `__/5`

## Dimension 2 — TAM and Runway

Total Addressable Market (TAM) size matters, but TAM capture trajectory matters more.

Questions:
- What is the current penetration rate of the relevant product or service in its total market?
- Is the TAM itself growing (secular expansion), stable, or shrinking (secular contraction)?
- At current growth rates, how many years until the company approaches market saturation?
- Is the company's share of TAM growing, stable, or eroding?

Watch for:
- **Top-down TAM inflation:** Analyst TAM estimates are frequently 3–10x the realistically addressable market. Stress-test by building from the bottom up.
- **Runway compression:** A company growing fast in a small TAM may saturate within 3 years; model the deceleration.

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What substitution effect (new technology, behavioral shift, regulatory constraint) compresses this TAM within 10 years?

Score: `__/5`

---

## Dimension 3 — Secular Tailwind Alignment

This dimension checks whether the company's business model is aligned with, or exposed against, the confirmed megatrends from the checklist.

Questions:
- Which megatrend category does this company benefit from directly? Indirectly?
- If the megatrend stalls (see disconfirmation prompts in megatrends-checklist), how exposed is the company's revenue?
- Are there any secular headwinds this company faces that could offset tailwinds (e.g., a clean-energy company with a coal legacy, a healthcare company facing drug pricing reform)?

Score: `__/5`

---

## Dimension 4 — Balance Sheet Resilience

A 10-year thesis requires the company to survive the intervening bear markets, credit crunches, and industry dislocations.

Questions:
- What is the net debt / EBITDA ratio? What does it look like under a stress scenario (revenue -30%, margins compressed)?
- Does the company generate free cash flow (FCF)? What is the FCF yield? Is FCF growing?
- What is the debt maturity profile? Are there near-term maturities that require refinancing at potentially higher rates?
- Does the company have access to liquidity (revolver, cash on hand) to survive a 2-year revenue downturn?

Score: `__/5`

---

## Dimension 5 — Management and Capital Allocation

Over a 10-year horizon, capital allocation quality often matters more than current business quality.

Questions:
- What is the track record of capital allocation decisions: M&A, buybacks, dividends, R&D investment? How do acquisitions look 5 years later?
- Does management own significant equity aligned with long-term outcomes, or is compensation primarily short-term cash/options?
- Has management been transparent in past communications, including during downturns? Do they deliver on stated milestones?
- Is there key-person risk? What is the succession plan?

**Disconfirmation prompt:** What capital allocation error (overpriced acquisition, excessive leverage, share dilution) has this management made in the past that might recur?

Score: `__/5`

---

## Dimension 6 — Disruption Risk

This is the most frequently underweighted factor at a 10-year horizon. Industries that looked impregnable in 2010 (retail, media, energy, banking) faced existential disruption by 2020.

Questions:
- What technological, regulatory, or business-model disruption has the potential to commoditize or obsolete this company's core product?
- Is there a venture-backed or incumbent-backed competitor building a 10x better alternative right now? What is their funding and progress?
- What is the company's own investment in disruption-proofing (R&D as % of revenue, internal innovation programs, startup acquisitions)?
- Are there signs of disruption already visible in the data — slowing customer acquisition, rising churn, margin pressure from new entrants?

**Disconfirmation prompt:** If you were running a startup with $500M to disrupt this company, what would you build? Does that attack surface already have funded players?

Score: `__/5` (5 = very low disruption risk; 0 = severe and near-term disruption risk)

**Interpretation (rough guide only):**
- 24–30: High conviction on durability — proceed to valuation sanity.
- 16–23: Moderate — thesis has meaningful fragilities; document them explicitly and size position conservatively.
- 8–15: Low durability — either avoid, or build a concentrated bear case as a hedge.
- Below 8: Severe concerns — thesis as stated is fragile.

**Minimum bar:** Any single dimension scoring 0 or 1 should be treated as a thesis-threatening concern, regardless of total score.
