## When to Use This Skill

Invoke this skill when you need to:

- Research whether a theme, sector, or asset class has genuine 10-year durability.
- Stress-test an investment thesis against disconfirming evidence and second-order effects.
- Run a structured valuation sanity check (not a precise price target).
- Think through position sizing, diversification, and behavioral risks.
- Log a thesis in a decision journal so you can review your reasoning later.

Do **not** use this skill to get a buy/sell recommendation, a price target, or portfolio construction advice tailored to your personal circumstances.

---

## Core Principles

1. **Process over predictions.** Ten-year forecasts are almost always wrong in specifics. Good process — identifying durable tailwinds, avoiding fatal risks, sizing bets appropriately — is more controllable than predicting outcomes.
2. **Base rates first.** Before building a bull case, ask: "What is the historical base rate for this kind of investment thesis working out?" Most disruption stories, most turnaround stories, and most commodity super-cycles disappoint.
3. **Seek disconfirmation.** Spend at least as much time looking for evidence that your thesis is wrong as looking for evidence it is right. A thesis that cannot be falsified is a belief, not an analysis.
4. **Second-order effects matter.** The first-order effect is usually priced in. What happens downstream — to suppliers, competitors, regulators, consumer behavior — is where insight lives.
5. **Humility is a feature.** Express views as probability ranges, not certainties. Document your key assumptions explicitly so you can track which ones broke.

## End-to-End Research Workflow

### Step 1 — Frame the Thesis Clearly

State, in two sentences or fewer:
- What is the thing you are researching (asset, company, sector, theme)?
- What is the core claim — why should it be worth significantly more in 10 years?

Use the **`references/decision-journal-template.md`** to capture this before going further.

### Step 2 — Megatrend Validation

Open **`references/megatrends-checklist.md`**.

Run through each category to determine whether the thesis is anchored to a durable secular force or a cyclical/fad dynamic. Apply the disconfirmation prompts. A thesis that cannot survive this checklist should be reconsidered or narrowed.

Key questions:
- Is this demographic, energy, compute, or biotech-driven — or is it sentiment/narrative-driven?
- Would this trend have been plausible 10 years ago? Why didn't it happen then?
- What would have to be true for the trend to reverse within 5 years?

### Step 3 — Durability Scoring

Open **`references/durability-rubric.md`**.

Score the specific asset or company on each dimension using the provided rubric. Sum the scores. This does not produce a buy/sell signal — it surfaces where the thesis is strong and where it is fragile.

Areas requiring particular scrutiny:
- Disruption risk (often the most underweighted factor at a 10-year horizon)
- Capital allocation discipline (compounding depends on this)
- Balance sheet resilience (can it survive a 5-year bear scenario?)

### Step 4 — Valuation Sanity Check

Open **`references/valuation-sanity.md`**.

Do NOT build a precise DCF. Instead:
- Run the reverse-DCF: what growth rate is implied by the current price? Is that plausible given base rates?
- Compute owner-earnings yield and compare to alternatives.
- Build three scenarios (bear / base / bull) and check whether the bear case is survivable.
- Identify the margin of safety: how wrong can you be and still achieve a reasonable outcome?

### Step 5 — Risk and Sizing

Open **`references/risk-and-sizing.md`**.

Before any position sizing decision:
- What is your maximum tolerable drawdown on this position?
- What correlation does it carry to your existing holdings?
- What behavioral risks apply (narrative attachment, recency bias, illiquidity trap)?

A position size that causes you to check prices daily is too large.

### Step 6 — Log the Decision

Return to **`references/decision-journal-template.md`** and complete every field.

Set a calendar reminder for your first review date (typically 12–18 months out). The journal exists so that future-you has enough information to evaluate whether the thesis broke — or you just experienced normal volatility.

## A Note on Forecasting Humility

The research community has documented repeatedly (Tetlock, Kahneman, Taleb) that expert forecasters performing at 10-year horizons barely outperform base rates, and often underperform simple heuristics. This framework does not promise to beat markets. It aims to:

- Reduce obvious errors (buying fads, ignoring disruption risk, over-sizing).
- Surface your actual assumptions so you can track which ones broke.
- Build a process that improves through documented learning rather than hindsight rationalization.

Markets are complex adaptive systems. Treat every analysis as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.
